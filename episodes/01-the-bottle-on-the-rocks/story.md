# Episode 1 — The Bottle on the Rocks

**Series**: *The Lighthouse Letters*

**Art style (primary, daily)**: Risograph
**Art styles (showcase only)**: Graphic Novel, Cyanotype
**Target pages**: 10
**Word count**: ~2,850

The fog had not lifted in three days. Hannah Cole stood at the kitchen window of the Keeper's House and watched it move — slow, undecided — across the granite below Wren's Head Light. The lighthouse beam swept through it every twelve seconds, useless and beautiful at once. Somewhere out past the harbor mouth, the foghorn at Pickerel Point gave its low two-note groan, and a moment later the lighthouse's own horn answered, deeper and slower, the way an older animal answers a younger one.

She had been awake since four. The cottage made small sounds she did not yet know how to interpret: a creak in the joists when the wind shifted, a sigh from the chimney flue, the quiet ticking of a clock she had not been able to find in any room. Hannah was twenty-eight years old. She was wearing dark jeans she had slept in, leather boots already laced — she had not been able to decide whether she was going outside — and Adelaide's cream fisherman sweater, the one with the cuff her grandmother had darned with the wrong color thread. The kitchen smelled of woodsmoke, salt, and old paper. Six weeks ago, Hannah had been a graphic designer in a glass-walled office in Boston. She still flinched at silence.

She caught her own reflection in the dark glass of the window and almost did not recognize herself. Wavy honey-brown hair, chin-length, gone a little wild from sleep. Pale skin, the freckles across her nose more visible in the cold light. The face of someone who had not yet decided whether to stay.

She turned away from the window and poured coffee into Adelaide's chipped white mug.

The kitchen was the smallest room in the cottage and the one she had begun to feel was hers. A black iron woodstove in the corner, its glass door blackened with use. A scarred pine table she had sat at most of yesterday afternoon without doing anything. Open shelves along the back wall lined with cookbooks, all of them earmarked at different recipes — Adelaide had been the kind of cook who annotated. A blue enamel kettle on the stovetop. A pair of wire-rim reading glasses still folded on the windowsill where Adelaide had left them the morning she went into the hospital, and which Hannah had not been able to bring herself to move.

The tabby cat watched her from the rug by the woodstove. He had broad orange-and-black mackerel stripes, a white chin, and the particular expression of an animal who has been here longer than you have. He had not yet decided about her either. Hannah had not yet named him. To name him felt like staying.

In the corner of the room sat the chest. Cedar, brass-cornered, the size of a small steamer trunk, locked with a key she had found taped beneath the kitchen drawer on her fourth day in the house. She had opened it once, on the fifth day, and closed it again without looking inside. Six weeks of avoidance had begun to feel like a decision in itself.

This morning, for reasons she could not articulate, she crossed the kitchen, set the coffee on the table, and lifted the lid.

The smell came up first — cedar oil, old paper, a thread of something herbal she would later identify as lavender. Inside the chest were letters. Hundreds of them. Stacked, sorted, bundled, sealed. The envelopes had yellowed at different rates, as if they had been written across decades; some were crisp and recent, others soft with age, a few bordered in black like old mourning notices. They were tied in bundles with brown twine, and each bundle bore a small paper tag in Adelaide's hand: a year, a single word, sometimes nothing at all.

Hannah reached in and took the topmost envelope from the topmost bundle. The paper was thick — rag-stock, the kind you could not buy at an office store. The handwriting on the front was Adelaide's, neat and slanting and unmistakable.

*To the next one. — A.*

She held it. She did not open it. Her mouth had gone dry without her noticing. She turned the envelope over once, ran her thumb along the unbroken seal, and slid it back into the bundle exactly as she had found it. She closed the chest. She stood up. The cat had moved without her seeing him move and was now sitting on the chest, watching her, his tail flicking once at the tip.

She went outside.

The path to the rocks ran from the kitchen door down a slope of wet grass, across a stretch of bleached driftwood that someone — Adelaide, presumably — had arranged into a kind of stepping-stone causeway, and out onto the granite of the headland itself. The granite was streaked dark with damp, patched with bladderwrack and the pale crunch of small white shells underfoot. The tide was halfway out. Somewhere offshore in the fog, a lobster boat was running its engine — she could hear it idle, then rev, then idle again, but she could not see it. The smell was sea: salt, iodine, and the green vegetal smell of seaweed in the sun.

Hannah walked with her hands in the pockets of her olive parka. The cold sting on her face felt good. She had almost made it back to the foot of the cottage path when something in the corner of her eye made her stop and turn.

It was wedged between two granite slabs where the last high tide had left it. Green glass. The cork sealed with red wax. Hannah crouched. The wax was cracked but holding. There was a folded paper inside.

She picked it up. The glass was cold from the sea. She carried it back to the cottage.

In the kitchen, with the woodstove ticking and the coffee gone cold, she set the bottle on the table and looked at it for a long moment before she did anything else. Then she took the butter knife from beside the bread box and worked the wax loose around the cork. She had to tip the bottle and tap it twice to get the paper out — it was a single folded sheet, the same thick rag-stock as the envelope in the chest, and her fingernails caught on it before it slid free.

She unfolded the letter.

The handwriting was not Adelaide's. It was not anyone's she knew. It was a careful hand, slightly rounded, the letters evenly spaced, as if the writer had taken time over each word.

*Hannah Cole, c/o The Light —*

*I know you don't believe in this yet. Here is what you need to know about Tuesday. A man named Ben Whitfield will hike north along the cliff path. He will reach the place where the trail bends above the cormorant rocks. The fog will be thick enough that he will not see the edge. He has a wife named Renee and a daughter named Mia and he has not told either of them where he is going today.*

*You have until three o'clock.*

Hannah read it twice. The kitchen ticked. The cat, who had not been on her lap a moment ago, was now on her lap.

She read it a third time and then set the letter flat on the table in front of her and did not move for a full minute. Her hands were cold. The cottage made a small settling sound. Outside, the foghorn at Pickerel Point gave its two-note groan, and the lighthouse horn answered.

*Cormorant rocks.* She knew where that was — she had walked there once, in better weather, with Adelaide. Above the cliff. Maybe forty minutes north along the path.

She did not know anyone named Ben Whitfield.

* * *

Far north of the lighthouse, on a stretch of trail Hannah had not yet walked, a man in a red windbreaker stopped at the shoulder of the path to check a paper map. He was in his early forties, broad-shouldered, six feet tall in his hiking boots, dark hair greying at the temples and at the close-cropped beard. He was wearing the windbreaker zipped to his throat, hiking pants, and a small daypack. The paper map was folded the wrong way — he had refolded it twice already and given up on neatness.

Ben Whitfield had woken at five that morning in his house in Augusta, ninety miles inland. He had told his wife Renee that he had a contractor meeting in Portland and would be back by dinner. He had not had a contractor meeting in Portland. He had taken the wrong exit deliberately, two hours ago, and had been driving the coast road since with a vague, persistent sense that there was something he needed to look at. He had not yet been able to articulate to himself what.

His phone's GPS had stopped working three minutes ago, and he had stepped off into the trees to find a trail marker. He could not find one. The fog was thicker than he had expected. He folded the map and stepped forward, his boots cracking small twigs, looking for the wooden post he was sure he had seen from the road. The path ahead curved out of sight into white nothing. He did not look down at his feet.

He did not yet know that the trail bent sharply to the left in about a hundred yards, and that the ground to the right of that bend dropped eighty feet straight down into a cluster of cormorant-streaked granite teeth.

He thought about his daughter Mia, who had asked him, the night before, why he seemed so quiet at dinner. He had said he was tired. He had been lying. He kept walking.

* * *

The knock at the cottage door came at half past nine. Three quick taps, a pause, one more.

Hannah opened the door and the cold air rushed in around her ankles. Pastor Tom Whittaker stood on the step. He was tall and weathered, late sixties, his white hair cropped short and combed neatly back, his blue-grey eyes set deep above the kind of crow's-feet you earn from forty years of looking out over water in winter. He was wearing his navy parka over a black shirt with a white clerical collar at the throat. In his right hand he held a covered casserole dish wrapped in a clean blue tea towel; the smell of warm chicken pie drifted up from it, butter and thyme and something herbal she could not name.

He did not say good morning.

He looked at her face. Then he looked at the letter, which she had forgotten she was still holding.

She watched his expression change. First the small flicker of recognition — he had seen this before, or seen something like it. Then, beneath the recognition, a shadow of something that took her a second to identify: grief. He had loved Adelaide for forty years, and the fact of the letter in Hannah's hand was Adelaide gone, all over again, in a way that the funeral itself had somehow not quite been.

"Your grandmother told me to expect this day," he said. His voice was quieter than she remembered from the service. "She said it would come about three weeks after she was gone." He paused. The fog moved between them. "It's been six."

Hannah opened her mouth. Closed it. She did not know what to ask first.

"Tom — " she began.

He held up his free hand, very gently, the way one would steady a small bird, and did not let her finish.

"I'm not the one to tell you. Adelaide was very clear about that." He stepped past her into the cottage just far enough to set the casserole on the counter inside the door. He did not take off his coat. He did not sit down. He did not come further. "There's a chicken pie in there. Eat something."

He turned to go. At the porch step he paused and looked back. His mouth did something that was not quite a smile.

"Whatever you decide, Hannah — don't decide it on an empty stomach."

He walked down the path toward the road, slowly, his hands back in his pockets. The fog took him before he reached the gate.

* * *

Hannah closed the door slowly. She stood with her back against it for a long time. She looked at the letter in her hand. She looked at the chest. She looked out the front window at the white nothing where the sea should have been. The cat made a small interrogative sound from somewhere behind her. She did not answer him.

She read the letter for the fourth time. She read it for the fifth.

She thought about not going.

There was a version of this morning, she understood, in which she folded the letter back along its creases, put it into the cedar chest with all the others, made herself eat some of Pastor Tom's chicken pie, and spent the rest of the day cleaning out the back bedroom upstairs, the one Adelaide had used as a study. By tomorrow she could have it half-painted. By next week she could have made the cottage feel like hers. And whatever the system was that had produced this letter — whatever Adelaide had been doing for the past forty years, whatever Pastor Tom knew and would not say — could go on without her, or could quietly stop. She had not asked for any of this. She did not owe Saltgrove anything. She did not owe her grandmother's secret life a single morning of her own.

She thought about Ben Whitfield, who she did not know, who had a daughter named Mia.

She thought about her grandmother, who had once told her — Hannah had been maybe ten years old, standing on this same kitchen floor — *the world doesn't ask you nicely, sweetheart. It asks you the way it asks. And you can answer or you can pretend you didn't hear, but those are the two choices.*

The cat had jumped onto the table and was sitting beside the letter, very still, watching her.

"Fine," Hannah said aloud, to the cat. "Fine."

She put on her boots.

* * *

The cliff path ran north from the cottage along the spine of the headland, climbing in slow switchbacks through scrub pine and bayberry, the wooden trail markers half-swallowed by the fog. Hannah walked with her hands in her parka pockets and the letter folded against her ribs. The path was wet. Her boots crunched on shell-strewn dirt and then on bare granite. She could see maybe fifteen feet ahead of her at any time. The world had been reduced to a circle of damp ground and the next white step into it.

She passed the first switchback, then the second. The trail climbed. She did not see a single other person. After a while she heard, distantly, the slow groan of the foghorn at Pickerel Point, and it was the only sound in the world that was not her own breathing.

She came at last to the wooden gate that marked the start of the headland trail proper — the part that ran along the cliff edge above the cormorant rocks. The gate was weathered grey, the brass latch tarnished. A small carved sign on the post read CAUTION — CLIFF EDGE — STAY ON MARKED TRAIL. Hannah put her hand on the latch and held it there.

She was, she realized, very afraid.

"It's only Tuesday," she said aloud, to nobody, because there was nobody for half a mile in any direction. "Maybe I'm just going for a walk."

The latch clicked open.

* * *

That evening, the lights of Saltgrove came on in their windows one by one. The lighthouse beam swept the fog, twelve seconds on, twelve seconds off, as it had since 1827. The foghorn at Pickerel Point gave its slow two-note groan, and the lighthouse horn answered.

At the corner of Pickerel Wharf, the diner glowed warm against the dusk. Inside, Dot Bergstrom stood behind the counter — early seventies, robust, her long grey braid down her back, a white cotton apron over a faded red-plaid flannel shirt rolled to the elbows. She held her chipped white coffee mug in one hand and the glass coffee pot in the other, and she was pouring a refill for a man at the counter who had not asked for one. The man took it without comment. Dot moved on.

Out at the harbor, Cal Hartley pulled the door of the bait shop shut behind him, snapped the padlock through the hasp, and walked home along the wharf with his hands in his pockets. He was thirty-four, tousled honey-brown hair under a brown beanie pulled low, light stubble, blue eyes. He wore a white tee under a red-and-black plaid flannel shirt and a battered canvas work jacket over it; his jeans were stained at the cuffs from a morning of cleaning bait barrels. The single silver ring on his right thumb caught the lamplight as he walked. He had not spoken to Hannah Cole yet. He had heard, from Dot, that she was at the lighthouse. He did not yet know that he would meet her, properly, on Thursday.

At the rectory of St. Brendan's, Pastor Tom Whittaker stood at his front window with both hands in the pockets of his navy parka and watched the lighthouse beam sweep the fog. He did not move for a long time. At last he said something quietly to himself that nobody heard.

And out in the dark water, a quarter mile from the buoy at the harbor mouth, a second bottle rode in slowly on the tide. Green glass. The cork sealed with red wax. It bumped once against the buoy chain and continued on.

Saltgrove had been quiet for six weeks.

It would not be quiet for long.
